# Thinking Like Your Opponent: 6 Strategic AI Prompts for Business Intelligence

**Author:** @alex_prompter

**Date:** 2026-04-14
**Type:** bookmark
**Source:** [Tweet Thread](https://x.com/alex_prompter/status/2043992940855615716)

## Summary

This thread opens with a reference to Bobby Fischer's legendary approach to chess—mentally occupying his opponent's perspective before the game even began. The author argues this same principle, applied systematically through AI, can give entrepreneurs a decisive edge across every business interaction.

The thread presents six distinct role-prompt templates, each casting the AI as a different type of intelligence specialist. Rather than asking AI to generate content or solve problems directly, these prompts ask it to first *map the psychology* of relevant parties—the client who might object, the competitor optimizing for certain outcomes, the partner with hidden incentives, the audience member prone to scrolling past. The premise is that superior information about human motivation precedes superior strategy.

The framework spans the full arc of business relationships: from pre-sales client mapping and competitor prediction, through partnership and negotiation preparation, to audience psychology for content creators, culminating in an integrated "strategic intelligence architect" role that makes opponent-thinking automatic rather than ad hoc. The author positions these as freely accessible tools for solopreneurs who want to consistently outperform better-resourced competitors by thinking more strategically about human behavior.

## Key Takeaways

- [[Strategic thinking]] borrowed from elite competitive domains (chess) can be systematized through structured AI role-prompting
- [[Client intelligence]] gathered *before* sales conversations reveals objections, hesitations, and unstated fears that shape deal outcomes
- [[Competitive intelligence]] requires understanding not just what competitors *do*, but what they're *optimizing for* and what constraints they operate under
- [[Partnership failure]] often stems from one party designing arrangements without fully mapping the other party's payoffs and fears
- [[Audience psychology]] for content creators involves mapping the specific moments that cause stopping, scrolling, or saving behavior
- [[Negotiation preparation]] framed as intelligence-gathering (payoffs, pressure points, fears) shifts the advantage from who has the best argument to who understood the terrain first
- Systematic application of these perspectives should become an *automatic habit* integrated into significant decisions
