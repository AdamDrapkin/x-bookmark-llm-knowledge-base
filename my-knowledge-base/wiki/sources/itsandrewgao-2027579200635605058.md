## Summary

This tweet makes a sharp observation about the [[vibecoding]] workflow: the quality of AI-generated frontend code is directly tied to the developer's vocabulary for [[ui-components]]. The author argues that when users can only articulate requests using basic terms like "menu" and "button," they're destined to get generic, uninspired outputs—what he calls "slop."

The underlying insight is that [[claude-opus]] (and similar AI models) are highly responsive to precise terminology. When you know terms like *breadcrumb navigation*, *pagination controls*, *accordion panels*, *skeleton loaders*, *toast notifications*, or *command palettes*, you unlock the model's ability to generate exactly the component you envisioned. Without this vocabulary, you're essentially asking a skilled designer to make something "good looking" without any direction.

This connects to broader discussions in the [[ai-productivity]] space about how [[prompt-engineering]] is less about magic words and more about domain expertise. The better your mental model of what's possible, the better your prompts—and thus your outputs.

## Key Takeaways

- **Vocabulary = leverage**: Learning component names isn't just about communication; it's about accessing the full design space
- **Generic prompts → generic results**: Terms like "menu" and "button" give AI too much interpretive freedom, leading to bland defaults
- **Domain expertise matters more than prompting tricks**: Deep knowledge of your domain (UI/UX in this case) is the highest-leverage skill
- **Vibecoding has a learning curve**: Even with AI assistance, you need foundational knowledge to direct it effectively
- **AI amplifies what you already know**: You can't vibecode what you can't articulate
