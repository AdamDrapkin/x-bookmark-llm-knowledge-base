## Summary

This tweet narrates a scathing critique of BetterHelp's business model, presenting it as a betrayal of vulnerable users. The thread walks through the typical user journey: someone struggling with mental health downloads a therapy app, pays a premium for confidentiality, shares their deepest trauma and diagnoses, only to have that data sold to social media giants. The irony, the tweet implies, is that the very "therapist" users trusted was actually a data extraction mechanism. This critique gained significant traction, highlighting the growing concern that mental health apps market themselves as safe spaces while operating as data harvesting operations. The $360/month figure suggests annual pricing ($30/month × 12), though pricing varies by plan. The reference to "2 million people" doing the same underscores the scale of potential data exposure. This narrative connects to documented FTC action against BetterHelp for exactly these practices—revealing that the concerns raised here aren't hypothetical but reflect verified misconduct.

## Key Takeaways

- Mental health apps like BetterHelp collect highly sensitive data including diagnoses, trauma history, and fears—information users share in moments of vulnerability
- Users often pay premium prices specifically under the assumption their mental health data will remain private and protected
- Despite paying for privacy, documented cases show BetterHelp shared user data with Facebook, Snapchat, and other third parties for advertising purposes
- The FTC has taken action against BetterHelp for these exact practices, confirming the thread's core accusation reflects reality
- An estimated 2 million users (or similar scale) were affected, making this not an isolated incident but a systemic privacy failure at scale
- The thread exposes a fundamental contradiction: mental health apps position themselves as safe, therapeutic spaces while simultaneously monetizing the most intimate details of users' psychological lives
