# Karpathy's LLM-Powered Personal Knowledge Base System

## Summary

This tweet synthesizes a workflow that [[Andrej Karpathy]] has adopted for personal knowledge management, where he delegates the organization of research materials entirely to an [[LLM]]. Unlike traditional [[Zettelkasten]] or [[personal knowledge management]] systems that require manual linking and categorization, this approach uses the LLM as an active organizer that reads raw sources and generates a structured [[Obsidian]] wiki automatically. The core insight is that you never write the wiki yourself—the model writes everything, and you simply steer by asking questions and filing outputs back into the system. Over time, the knowledge base compounds because every answer and generated output becomes part of the searchable corpus.

The system operates as a five-stage pipeline: collect raw sources into a folder, let the LLM organize them into structured markdown files, query the wiki with complex research questions, render outputs in various formats (markdown, slides, charts), and file everything back to strengthen future queries. This creates a self-reinforcing loop where the more you use it, the smarter it becomes.

## Key Takeaways

- The fundamental shift is from using AI to *write code* to using it to *organize knowledge*—a move from generation to curation and retrieval
- The LLM handles all wiki writing and linking; humans only steer through questions and file management
- The system works without complex retrieval pipelines—a simple folder of markdown files and an LLM that maintains its own index is sufficient for ~100 articles and ~400K words
- Knowledge compounds because outputs from queries get filed back into the wiki, making future queries richer
- Tools involved include [[Obsidian]], Obsidian Web Clipper, [[Marp]] for slide generation, and [[Matplotlib]] for visualization
- Sources can include articles, papers, repositories, datasets, and images—all organized into a `raw/` folder initially

## Extracted Content

> **ANDREJ KARPATHY**
> How LLMs Turn Raw Research Into a Living Knowledge Base
>
> **CORE INSIGHT**
> You never write the wiki. The LLM writes everything. You just steer — and every answer compounds.
>
> **MAIN PIPELINE**
>
> **STEP 01 — COLLECT SOURCES**
> Dump articles, papers, repos, datasets, and images into a `raw/` folder. Use Obsidian Web Clipper to save articles as markdown with images downloaded locally.
>
> **STEP 02 — LLM ORGANIZES WIKI**
> The LLM reads everything in `raw/` and builds a structured wiki of `.md` files — summaries, concept articles, and links between related ideas across all your sources.
>
> **STEP 03 — ASK QUESTIONS**
> Query the wiki with complex questions. The LLM reads its own index files and finds answers across your entire collection. No complex retrieval system needed.
>
> **STEP 04 — RENDER OUTPUTS**
> Outputs go beyond text — the LLM renders markdown files, Marp slide decks, Matplotlib charts, and images. All viewable directly inside Obsidian.
>
> **STEP 05 — FILE BACK TO WIKI**
> Every output gets filed back into the wiki. Every question you ask makes the knowledge base smarter. The loop compounds — your research builds on itself.

## Relation to Other Wiki Pages

This workflow represents a practical evolution of [[personal knowledge management]] concepts applied specifically to AI-assisted research. It contrasts with traditional [[Zettelkasten]] approaches that emphasize human-authored atomic notes and manual linking. The emphasis on the LLM maintaining its own index rather than relying on external retrieval systems (like [[vector databases]]) places this closer to [[prompt-based retrieval]] paradigms. It also relates to the broader idea of [[LLMs as agents]] that can take ownership over organizing digital spaces.
